# Pitch Deck Outline

> A slide-by-slide guide to building an investor pitch deck that tells a compelling story and closes rounds.

---

## Deck Structure Overview

**Total slides:** 12-15 (no more than 20)

**Presentation time:** 15-20 minutes (leave time for Q&A)

**Rule of thumb:** 1-2 minutes per slide maximum

---

## Slide 1: Title

### What to include

- Company name + logo
- One-line tagline/description
- Your name + title
- Date
- **"Confidential"** (if fundraising)

### Example

> **[Company Name]**  
> *[One-sentence value prop — e.g., "AI-powered customer health platform that predicts churn 90 days in advance"]*  
> 
> [Your Name], CEO  
> [Date]  
> Confidential

---

## Slide 2: The Problem

### Objective

Make the investor *feel* the pain your customer experiences. If they don't understand why this problem matters, the rest of the deck won't land.

### What to include

- **3 specific pain points** your target customer faces
- **Quantify the problem** if possible (time wasted, money lost, frustration level)
- **Current workarounds** and why they fail
- Optional: Customer quote that captures the pain

### Storytelling tip

Start with a specific scenario: *"Imagine you're a VP of Customer Success at a 200-person SaaS company..."*

### Example structure

> **The Problem**
> 
> Customer success teams are flying blind:
> 
> 1. **Churn is reactive** — You only find out customers are unhappy after they cancel
> 2. **Data is scattered** — Customer health signals are buried across Salesforce, Zendesk, product analytics, and NPS surveys
> 3. **CSMs are overwhelmed** — Each CSM manages 50+ accounts and can't spot at-risk customers manually
> 
> *Result: 15% annual churn, $5M in lost ARR, and constant firefighting.*

---

## Slide 3: The Solution

### Objective

Show how your product elegantly solves the problem. Don't feature-dump — focus on the core insight or approach.

### What to include

- **High-level description** of what you do (1-2 sentences)
- **How it works** (the "magic" or key insight)
- **3 core capabilities** that directly address the 3 problems from Slide 2
- Optional: Product screenshot or demo video (30 sec max)

### Example structure

> **The Solution**
> 
> [Product Name] predicts which customers will churn 90 days before it happens.
> 
> **How it works:**
> - Connects to your existing tools (Salesforce, Zendesk, analytics)
> - ML model analyzes 200+ behavioral signals
> - Surfaces at-risk accounts with recommended actions
> 
> → CSMs know exactly who needs attention and why.

---

## Slide 4: Why Now?

### Objective

Explain the market timing. Why is this the right moment for your solution? What's changed that makes this possible or necessary now?

### What to include

- **Macro trends** (e.g., economic pressure forcing companies to focus on retention)
- **Technology enablers** (e.g., AI/ML maturity, API ecosystems)
- **Behavior changes** (e.g., remote work, digital-first)
- **Regulatory/competitive shifts**

### Example

> **Why Now?**
> 
> 1. **Economic pressure** — In a downturn, acquiring new customers is 5-7x more expensive than retaining existing ones. Boards are demanding retention improvements.
> 2. **Data infrastructure matured** — Companies finally have the data pipes (Segment, Fivetran) to connect all their customer signals.
> 3. **AI is accessible** — Predictive models that required data science teams 3 years ago can now be deployed via API.

---

## Slide 5: Market Size (TAM/SAM/SOM)

### Objective

Show that the market is big enough to build a venture-scale business, but focused enough that you have a wedge.

### What to include

- **TAM** (Total Addressable Market)
- **SAM** (Serviceable Addressable Market)
- **SOM** (Serviceable Obtainable Market — what you'll capture in 3-5 years)
- **Show your math** — cite sources (Gartner, IDC, bottoms-up calculation)

### Example

> **Market Size**
> 
> - **TAM:** $12B — Global B2B customer success software market (Gartner, 2025)
> - **SAM:** $2.4B — US mid-market B2B SaaS companies (50-500 employees) with dedicated CS teams
> - **SOM:** $120M — 5% market share achievable in 5 years based on [comparable company] trajectory
> 
> *Source: Gartner + bottom-up analysis*

---

## Slide 6: Product Demo / How It Works

### Objective

Show, don't tell. Give investors a visual sense of how the product works. Keep it high-level — save deep-dive for follow-up.

### What to include

- **3-4 screenshots** showing the core workflow
- **Annotated** with callouts explaining what's happening
- OR: **30-60 second screen recording** embedded (if presenting live, not PDF)

### Tip

Focus on the "aha moment" — the moment when value becomes obvious to the user.

---

## Slide 7: Business Model

### Objective

Explain how you make money. Simple, clear pricing model.

### What to include

- **Revenue model** (SaaS subscription, transaction fee, usage-based, etc.)
- **Pricing tiers** (if applicable)
- **Typical deal size** / ARPU
- **Contract length** (annual, multi-year?)
- **Expansion revenue** (upsells, add-ons, usage growth)

### Example

> **Business Model**
> 
> **SaaS subscription** — Annual contracts, paid upfront
> 
> | Tier | Price | Target Customer |
> |------|-------|-----------------|
> | **Growth** | $12K/year | 50-100 customers under management |
> | **Scale** | $36K/year | 100-300 customers |
> | **Enterprise** | $120K+/year | 300+ customers, custom features |
> 
> **Average deal size:** $48K  
> **Net revenue retention:** 120% (usage + upsells)

---

## Slide 8: Traction

### Objective

Prove that customers want this. The most important slide for early-stage investors.

### What to include

*Choose the metrics that tell the best story for your stage:*

**Pre-revenue / MVP:**
- Customer interviews completed
- Waitlist signups
- LOIs (letters of intent) or pilot commitments

**Early revenue ($0-500K ARR):**
- MRR / ARR growth chart
- # of paying customers
- Key logos

**Growth stage ($500K-5M ARR):**
- ARR growth (show the hockey stick)
- Gross retention / net retention
- CAC payback period
- Key customer wins

### Example (early stage)

> **Traction**
> 
> **$420K ARR** — 12 months post-launch
> 
> - 22 paying customers
> - 130% net revenue retention
> - 6-month CAC payback
> 
> **Key customers:** [Logo] [Logo] [Logo]
> 
> **Pipeline:** $1.2M in qualified opps (4x ARR)

---

## Slide 9: Competition / Competitive Advantage

### Objective

Show you understand the landscape and have a defensible position.

### What to include

- **2x2 matrix** or comparison table showing you vs. competitors
- **Your unique advantage** — what do you have that's hard to replicate?
- Avoid saying "we have no competitors" — there's always a competitive alternative (even if it's "do nothing")

### Example

> **Competition**
> 
> | | **Predictive** | **Action-Oriented** |
> |---------------|------------|---------------|
> | **Us** | ✅ | ✅ |
> | **Salesforce** | ❌ | ❌ |
> | **Gainsight** | ⚠️ (basic) | ⚠️ |
> | **BI tools** | ❌ | ❌ |
> 
> **Our advantage:**
> - Proprietary churn prediction model (85% accuracy)
> - Built for CS teams, not data scientists
> - 2-week implementation vs. 3-month competitors

---

## Slide 10: Go-to-Market Strategy

### Objective

Show how you'll acquire customers efficiently and scale distribution.

### What to include

- **ICP** (Ideal Customer Profile)
- **Primary channels** (outbound, inbound, partnerships, PLG, etc.)
- **Sales model** (self-serve, inside sales, field sales)
- **CAC** and **CAC payback** (if you have the data)

### Example

> **Go-to-Market**
> 
> **ICP:** B2B SaaS companies, 50-500 employees, $5-50M ARR
> 
> **Channels:**
> 1. **Outbound** (60% of pipeline) — SDR team targeting VPs of CS
> 2. **Inbound** (30%) — SEO, content marketing, webinars
> 3. **Partnerships** (10%) — Salesforce AppExchange, CS communities
> 
> **Sales cycle:** 45 days  
> **CAC:** $18K  
> **Payback:** 6 months

---

## Slide 11: Team

### Objective

Show you're the right team to execute. Investors bet on founders as much as ideas.

### What to include

- **Headshots + names + titles** of founding team
- **Relevant experience** (1-2 bullets per person showing domain expertise)
- **Why this team?** — what's your unfair advantage? (e.g., "20 years combined in CS leadership roles", "Built and sold a company in this space", etc.)
- Optional: Key advisors or early hires

### Example

> **Team**
> 
> **[Founder 1 Name] — CEO**  
> - 10 years in customer success leadership  
> - Built CS org at [Company], scaled from 10 to 200 customers
> 
> **[Founder 2 Name] — CTO**  
> - ML engineer, ex-Google Brain  
> - Built predictive models for [use case]
> 
> **[Founder 3 Name] — Head of Product**  
> - Former PM at [SaaS company]  
> - Launched 3 B2B products, 50K+ users

---

## Slide 12: Financials / Projections

### Objective

Show the path to a big outcome. Investors want to see you've thought through the next 3-5 years.

### What to include

- **Revenue projections** (3-5 years)
- **Key assumptions** (growth rate, ACV, churn, headcount)
- **Unit economics** (CAC, LTV, gross margin)
- **Path to profitability** or next major milestone

### Example

> **Financials**
> 
> | Year | ARR | Customers | Team Size |
> |------|-----|-----------|-----------|
> | 2024 (actual) | $420K | 22 | 5 |
> | 2025 | $2.1M | 85 | 12 |
> | 2026 | $6.5M | 220 | 25 |
> | 2027 | $15M | 450 | 45 |
> 
> **Key assumptions:**
> - 200% YoY growth Years 1-2, 120% Year 3
> - $48K ACV, 10% annual churn
> - 40% gross margin

---

## Slide 13: The Ask

### Objective

Be crystal clear about what you're raising, why, and what it gets you.

### What to include

- **Round size** (e.g., "$2M seed round")
- **Use of funds** (how will you spend it? Hiring, product, marketing?)
- **Milestones** you'll hit with this capital
- **Timeline** (how long will this last? 18-24 months is standard)

### Example

> **The Ask**
> 
> **Raising: $2M seed round**
> 
> **Use of funds:**
> - 50% — Engineering (ship v2, integrations)
> - 30% — Sales & marketing (SDR team, demand gen)
> - 20% — Ops & G&A
> 
> **Milestones (18 months):**
> - $3M ARR
> - 100 customers
> - Profitability at $5M ARR (24 months)

---

## Slide 14: Closing Slide

### What to include

- Company name + logo
- Your contact info (email, phone)
- **"Thank you" + "Questions?"**

---

## Appendix (Optional Slides)

Have these ready but don't present unless asked:

- Detailed product roadmap
- Customer case studies / testimonials
- Detailed financial model (P&L)
- Competitive landscape deep-dive
- Market research sources
- Cap table (current ownership structure)

## Deck Design Tips

### Visual Consistency

- Use your brand colors, typography, and logo from `02-brand/visual-identity.md`
- Keep slides uncluttered — one idea per slide
- Use high-quality images (no cheesy stock photos)

### Data Visualization

- Charts > tables (show trends visually)
- Limit to 1-2 charts per slide
- Always label axes and units

### Typography

- Use large, readable fonts (24pt minimum for body text, 36pt+ for headlines)
- Avoid walls of text — use bullets, short sentences

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ **Too many slides** — If your deck is 30+ slides, you're losing them  
❌ **Feature dump** — Investors don't care about every feature, they care about outcomes  
❌ **No traction slide** — If you have any data, show it  
❌ **Unrealistic projections** — 10x growth YoY for 5 years straight is not credible  
❌ **Vague market size** — "Multi-billion dollar market" without sources is meaningless  
❌ **Ignoring competition** — "We have no competitors" is a red flag  
❌ **Weak team slide** — If your team doesn't have relevant experience, get advisors who do

## Deck Versions

You need multiple versions for different contexts:

### 1. **Teaser deck** (5-7 slides)

For cold outreach to investors. Hits the high points: Problem, Solution, Traction, Ask.

### 2. **Full pitch deck** (12-15 slides)

For in-person or Zoom meetings. This document.

### 3. **Email deck** (same slides, but self-explanatory)

Investors will forward your deck. Make sure it makes sense without you presenting. Add more context in speaker notes or text.

### 4. **Demo day deck** (10 slides, 3 minutes)

Ultra-tight version for accelerator demo days.
