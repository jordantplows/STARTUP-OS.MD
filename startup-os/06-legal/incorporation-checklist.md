## Pre-Incorporation Planning

### Business Fundamentals
- [ ] **Choose a business name**
  - Check availability in target state
  - Verify domain name availability
  - Search USPTO trademark database
  - Reserve name if necessary (most states allow 60-120 day reservation)
  
- [ ] **Define business purpose**
  - Draft mission statement
  - Identify primary business activities
  - Document short-term and long-term goals

- [ ] **Identify founders**
  - List all co-founders with contact information
  - Document founder roles and responsibilities
  - Discuss equity split and vesting schedules
  - Ensure all founders are legally able to participate

- [ ] **Determine initial capitalization**
  - Calculate startup capital needed
  - Identify funding sources (personal savings, friends & family, investors)
  - Document any pre-incorporation investments or loans

## Entity Selection

### C-Corporation (Recommended for VC-Backed Startups)

**Advantages:**
- Preferred by venture capital investors
- Unlimited shareholders
- Multiple share classes (common, preferred)
- Stock options for employees
- Established legal framework
- Easier to take public (IPO)

**Disadvantages:**
- Double taxation (corporate and dividend levels)
- More complex compliance requirements
- Higher formation and maintenance costs
- More formal governance structure required

**Best For:**
- Startups seeking venture capital funding
- Companies planning to scale rapidly
- Businesses with plans for eventual IPO or acquisition

---

### S-Corporation

**Advantages:**
- Pass-through taxation (avoids double taxation)
- Limited liability protection
- Can issue stock and have shareholders

**Disadvantages:**
- Maximum 100 shareholders
- Only one class of stock
- Shareholders must be U.S. citizens or residents
- Not suitable for VC funding
- Restrictions on ownership structure

**Best For:**
- Small to medium-sized businesses
- Companies with domestic ownership only
- Businesses prioritizing tax efficiency over growth capital

---

### LLC (Limited Liability Company)

**Advantages:**
- Flexible management structure
- Pass-through taxation
- Fewer formalities than corporations
- Flexible profit distribution

**Disadvantages:**
- Not preferred by venture capital investors
- Cannot issue stock options (can use profits interests)
- May be subject to self-employment taxes
- Harder to transfer ownership

**Best For:**
- Bootstrapped companies
- Service businesses
- Real estate ventures
- Companies not seeking institutional investment

---

### Benefit Corporation (B-Corp)

**Advantages:**
- Mission-driven legal structure
- Protects social/environmental mission
- Enhanced reputation with conscious consumers
- Legal protection for pursuing social goals

**Disadvantages:**
- Additional reporting requirements
- May complicate fundraising
- Not recognized in all states
- Must balance profit with purpose

**Best For:**
- Social enterprises
- Mission-driven startups
- Companies prioritizing impact alongside profit

## State Selection

### Delaware C-Corporation (Most Common for Startups)

**Advantages:**
- Well-established corporate law framework
- Court of Chancery (specialized business court)
- Flexible corporate governance rules
- Strong legal precedents
- Preferred by VCs and acquirers
- Expedited filing options available

**Considerations:**
- Must pay annual franchise tax ($450 minimum)
- Need registered agent in Delaware
- May need to register as foreign corporation in home state
- Total cost typically higher than home state incorporation

---

### Home State Incorporation

**Advantages:**
- Lower initial costs
- Easier to maintain registered agent
- Only one state's laws to comply with
- Local attorney familiarity

**Considerations:**
- May be less attractive to sophisticated investors
- Potentially less flexible corporate law
- May still need Delaware incorporation for future funding

---

### Other States to Consider

**Nevada:**
- No state corporate income tax
- Strong privacy protections
- No information sharing with IRS

**Wyoming:**
- Low fees and taxes
- Strong LLC laws
- Privacy protections

**California (If Operating There):**
- Must comply with CA law regardless of incorporation state if headquarters are in CA
- "Pseudo-foreign corporation" rules apply to out-of-state corporations doing business in CA

## Incorporation Process

### Step 1: Prepare Formation Documents

#### Certificate of Incorporation (Delaware) / Articles of Incorporation (Other States)
- [ ] Company name: [COMPANY NAME]
- [ ] Registered agent name and address
- [ ] Authorized shares structure:
  - Number of authorized common shares: _________________ (typically 10-15 million)
  - Number of authorized preferred shares: _________________ (typically 5-10 million)
  - Par value: $0.0001 or $0.00001 (low par value recommended)
- [ ] Incorporator name and address (can be anyone; typically lawyer or founder)
- [ ] Purpose clause (can be general: "engage in any lawful act or activity")
- [ ] Initial directors (if designating in certificate)

#### Filing Requirements
- [ ] Complete certificate/articles of incorporation
- [ ] Pay filing fee (varies by state: DE = $89 + $50 filing fee, expedited options available)
- [ ] File with Secretary of State (online, mail, or in-person)
- [ ] Choose processing speed:
  - Standard (7-10 business days)
  - Expedited (24 hours, additional fee)
  - Same day (if available, highest fee)

---

### Step 2: Appoint Registered Agent
- [ ] Select registered agent (individual or service)
- [ ] Ensure agent has physical address in state of incorporation
- [ ] Confirm agent acceptance
- [ ] Document registered agent information:
  - Name: _______________________
  - Address: _______________________
  - Phone: _______________________
  - Email: _______________________

**Registered Agent Services:**
- CT Corporation
- CSC (Corporation Service Company)
- Incfile
- Northwest Registered Agent
- Typically $100-300/year

---

### Step 3: Initial Board of Directors Meeting

#### Organizational Resolutions
- [ ] Adopt bylaws
- [ ] Elect officers (President, Secretary, Treasurer at minimum)
- [ ] Approve corporate seal (if desired, often optional)
- [ ] Approve stock certificate form
- [ ] Authorize issuance of initial shares to founders
- [ ] Approve Section 83(b) elections (if applicable)
- [ ] Adopt fiscal year (typically calendar year for startups)
- [ ] Approve initial banking resolutions
- [ ] Approve formation expenses
- [ ] Approve equity incentive plan (if ready)
- [ ] Approve indemnification of directors and officers
- [ ] Approve D&O insurance (if purchasing)

#### Meeting Minutes
- [ ] Document date, time, location (or virtual)
- [ ] Record attendees
- [ ] Document all resolutions and votes
- [ ] Obtain signatures from directors
- [ ] Store in corporate records book

## Post-Incorporation Actions

### Federal Tax Registration
- [ ] **Obtain Employer Identification Number (EIN)**
  - Apply online at IRS.gov (free, instant)
  - Or file Form SS-4 by mail/fax
  - Required for: bank accounts, hiring employees, tax filings
  - Store EIN securely

- [ ] **File for S-Corporation election (if applicable)**
  - File Form 2553 with IRS
  - Must file within 2 months and 15 days of incorporation (or beginning of tax year)
  - All shareholders must consent

---

### State Tax Registration
- [ ] Register with state tax authority
- [ ] Obtain state tax ID number
- [ ] Register for sales tax permit (if selling taxable goods/services)
- [ ] Register for employer withholding (if hiring employees)
- [ ] Understand state nexus requirements

---

### Business Licenses and Permits
- [ ] Research required federal licenses
- [ ] Research required state licenses
- [ ] Research required local licenses and permits
- [ ] Apply for industry-specific licenses:
  - Professional licenses
  - Health permits
  - Building permits
  - Zoning permits
  - Environmental permits

---

### Banking and Financial Setup
- [ ] **Open corporate bank account**
  - Bring: EIN letter, certificate of incorporation, bylaws, resolution authorizing account
  - Determine authorized signers
  - Consider separate operating and reserve accounts
  
- [ ] **Establish accounting system**
  - Choose accounting software (QuickBooks, Xero, Wave)
  - Set up chart of accounts
  - Establish bookkeeping procedures
  - Consider hiring accountant or bookkeeper
  
- [ ] **Set up payment processing**
  - Merchant account
  - Payment gateway (Stripe, PayPal, Square)
  - ACH processing if needed

---

### Corporate Records
- [ ] Create corporate records book (physical or digital)
- [ ] Store all formation documents
- [ ] Maintain stock ledger
- [ ] Keep meeting minutes and resolutions
- [ ] Track compliance deadlines
- [ ] Implement document retention policy

---

### Insurance
- [ ] **General liability insurance**
- [ ] **Professional liability insurance** (E&O)
- [ ] **Directors & Officers (D&O) insurance** (essential for funded startups)
- [ ] **Property insurance** (if applicable)
- [ ] **Cyber liability insurance** (for tech companies)
- [ ] **Workers' compensation** (required if employees)
- [ ] **Key person insurance** (optional)

---

### Intellectual Property
- [ ] **Trademark registration**
  - Search existing trademarks
  - File application with USPTO
  - Consider international protection
  
- [ ] **Patent evaluation**
  - Conduct prior art search
  - File provisional patent application (if applicable)
  - Plan for utility patent filing
  
- [ ] **Copyright registration**
  - Register significant works with U.S. Copyright Office
  - Document creation dates
  
- [ ] **Domain names and social media**
  - Register relevant domain names
  - Secure social media handles
  - Consider defensive registrations

## Equity and Stock Structure

### Authorized vs. Issued Shares

**Authorized Shares:**
- Total shares the company CAN issue (set in certificate of incorporation)
- Common structure:
  - 10,000,000 authorized common shares
  - 5,000,000 authorized preferred shares
- Can only be changed by amendment to certificate (requires board and shareholder approval)

**Issued Shares:**
- Shares actually distributed to stockholders
- Start conservative (typically 6-8 million common shares to founders/early team)
- Leaves room for employee option pool and future growth

---

### Founder Stock Issuance

#### Determine Equity Split
- [ ] Document equity allocation rationale
- [ ] Consider factors:
  - Initial idea contribution
  - Domain expertise
  - Time commitment
  - Capital contribution
  - Opportunity cost
  - Prior work on project
  - Future role and responsibilities

#### Common Equity Split Frameworks
- **Equal split:** Simple but may not reflect contributions
- **Contribution-based:** Weighted by past/future value added
- **Dynamic split:** Adjusts over time (complex, often problematic)
- **Negotiated:** Frank discussion and agreement

**Recommended Approach:** Use Mike Moyer's "Slicing Pie" framework or seek professional guidance

---

### Stock Purchase Agreements

- [ ] **Execute Stock Purchase Agreement for each founder**
  - Number of shares
  - Purchase price (typically par value: $0.0001 per share)
  - Total payment amount
  - Vesting terms
  - Acceleration provisions
  - Right of repurchase
  
- [ ] **Collect payment for shares**
  - Cash payment (document with receipt)
  - Promissory note (if permitted and agreed)
  - Past services (check state law, may be problematic)
  - Property transfer (document fair market value)

---

### Vesting Schedule

**Standard 4-Year Vest with 1-Year Cliff:**
- 25% vests after 1 year (cliff)
- Remaining 75% vests monthly over 36 months
- Total vesting period: 48 months
- If founder leaves before 1 year: forfeits all unvested shares

**Key Terms:**
- [ ] Vesting commencement date: [DATE]
- [ ] Total vesting period: _______ years
- [ ] Cliff period: _______ months
- [ ] Vesting frequency: Monthly / Quarterly
- [ ] Acceleration on acquisition: Single trigger / Double trigger / None
- [ ] Acceleration on termination without cause: Yes / No
- [ ] Acceleration on death or disability: Yes / No

**Alternative Vesting Structures:**
- Founders with significant prior work: Consider partial acceleration
- Equal co-founders: Symmetric vesting
- Founder-CEO: May negotiate different terms
- Advisors: Typically 2-year vest with no cliff or quarterly vesting

---

### Stock Restrictions and Agreements

#### Restricted Stock Agreement
- [ ] Vesting schedule clearly documented
- [ ] Company right to repurchase unvested shares
- [ ] Repurchase price (typically original purchase price)
- [ ] Repurchase mechanics (within 90 days of termination)
- [ ] Drag-along rights
- [ ] Tag-along rights
- [ ] Right of first refusal
- [ ] Restrictions on transfer

#### Founders' Stock Purchase Agreement
- [ ] All founders sign uniform agreement
- [ ] Clear vesting terms
- [ ] Repurchase rights
- [ ] IP assignment provisions
- [ ] Non-compete/non-solicit (if enforceable in jurisdiction)

---

### Section 83(b) Election

**CRITICAL: Must be filed within 30 days of stock grant**

**Purpose:**
- Elect to be taxed on stock value at grant date (typically par value) rather than at vesting
- Avoids ordinary income tax on vesting date appreciation
- Future appreciation taxed as capital gains (not ordinary income)
- Essential for founder stock with vesting

**Requirements:**
- [ ] Complete IRS Form 83(b) for each founder
- [ ] Include required information:
  - Stockholder name, address, SSN
  - Description of property (shares)
  - Date of transfer
  - Tax year
  - Number of shares
  - Restrictions on property
  - Fair market value
  - Amount paid
  - Copy of stock purchase agreement
  
- [ ] **File within 30 days of stock purchase (hard deadline, no extensions)**
- [ ] Mail to IRS address where you file tax returns (certified mail, return receipt)
- [ ] Send copy to [COMPANY NAME]
- [ ] Attach copy to founder's tax return for the year
- [ ] Keep proof of filing (certified mail receipt, return receipt)

**Consequences of Missing 83(b) Deadline:**
- Taxed on fair market value at each vesting date
- Tax due even without selling shares (no liquidity)
- Taxed at ordinary income rates (not capital gains)
- Can result in massive tax bills

**83(b) Election Template Location:** `/forms/83b-election-template.pdf`

### Stock Ledger and Capitalization Table

- [ ] Create and maintain stock ledger
  - Date of issuance
  - Stockholder name
  - Number of shares
  - Class of stock
  - Certificate number
  - Purchase price
  - Vesting schedule
  
- [ ] Set up capitalization table (cap table)
  - Use software: Carta, Pulley, AngelList
  - Track: ownership %, fully diluted %, option pool
  - Model future dilution scenarios
  - Update with every stock issuance

---

### Employee Option Pool

**Initial Pool Size:**
- Typically 10-20% of post-money capitalization
- Depends on hiring needs and VC expectations
- Created before Series A (often negotiated with lead investor)

**Option Plan Requirements:**
- [ ] Board approval of equity incentive plan
- [ ] Stockholder approval (typically unanimous at this stage)
- [ ] Choose plan type:
  - Incentive Stock Options (ISOs) - preferred, tax advantages
  - Non-Qualified Stock Options (NSOs)
- [ ] Set exercise price (typically FMV at grant date)
- [ ] Determine vesting schedule (typically 4 years with 1-year cliff)
- [ ] File 409A valuation (required for option grants)

## Tax Matters

### Initial Tax Elections and Filings

- [ ] **Choose fiscal year**
  - Calendar year (Jan 1 - Dec 31) - most common for startups
  - Or alternative fiscal year if business reasons justify
  
- [ ] **Choose accounting method**
  - Accrual method (required if inventory, generally recommended)
  - Cash method (simpler, if permitted)
  
- [ ] **File initial tax returns**
  - Federal: Form 1120 (C-corp) or 1120S (S-corp)
  - State: Corporate income tax return
  - Due: 15th day of 3rd month after fiscal year end (March 15 for calendar year S-corps, April 15 for C-corps)

---

### Ongoing Tax Compliance

**Federal:**
- [ ] Quarterly estimated tax payments (if owed)
- [ ] Annual corporate tax return
- [ ] Employment tax deposits (if employees)
- [ ] Form W-2 for employees (by January 31)
- [ ] Form 1099-NEC for contractors paid >$600 (by January 31)
- [ ] Form 1099-MISC for other payments (rent, etc.)

**State:**
- [ ] Annual franchise tax (Delaware: due by March 1)
- [ ] State income tax (if applicable)
- [ ] Sales tax filings (monthly/quarterly/annually depending on volume)
- [ ] Unemployment insurance tax

**Recordkeeping:**
- [ ] Maintain records for at least 7 years
- [ ] Document all business expenses
- [ ] Track mileage if using personal vehicle
- [ ] Save receipts and invoices
- [ ] Reconcile accounts monthly

---

### Tax-Advantaged Structures

#### QSBS (Qualified Small Business Stock)
- Potentially 100% exemption from capital gains tax on sale
- Requirements:
  - C-corporation
  - Gross assets <$50M at time of issuance
  - Active business (not passive investment)
  - Domestic corporation
  - Stock held >5 years
- [ ] Structure to qualify for QSBS
- [ ] Track holding periods
- [ ] Maintain QSBS documentation

#### R&D Tax Credits
- [ ] Evaluate eligibility for R&D tax credits
- [ ] Track qualified research expenses (QREs)
- [ ] Document research activities
- [ ] Consider payroll tax offset for startups (<$5M revenue, <5 years old)

## Compliance and Ongoing Requirements

### Annual State Compliance

#### Delaware C-Corporation
- [ ] **Annual Franchise Tax** (due March 1)
  - Minimum: $450
  - Based on authorized shares or assumed par value method
  - Calculate which method is cheaper
  - File online at Delaware Division of Corporations
  
- [ ] **Annual Report** (included with franchise tax)

#### Other States
- [ ] Check home state requirements
- [ ] File annual report (most states require)
- [ ] Pay annual fees/taxes
- [ ] Update registered agent if changed
- [ ] File any required beneficial ownership reports

---

### Corporate Governance

#### Annual Requirements
- [ ] **Annual stockholder meeting**
  - Elect directors
  - Approve significant corporate actions
  - Document in meeting minutes
  - Can be held virtually or by written consent
  
- [ ] **Regular board meetings**
  - Quarterly recommended
  - Approve major decisions
  - Review financial performance
  - Maintain minutes
  
- [ ] **Written consents in lieu of meetings**
  - Must be unanimous (unless bylaws provide otherwise)
  - Document all board and stockholder actions
  - Store in corporate records

#### Required Actions Needing Board Approval
- Stock issuance
- Option grants
- Significant contracts
- Loans or financing
- Change of registered agent
- Amendment to bylaws
- Amendment to certificate of incorporation (also needs stockholder approval)
- Approval of annual budget
- Hiring/firing officers
- Acquisitions or sales of assets

---

### Employment Compliance

#### If Hiring Employees
- [ ] **Federal requirements:**
  - Form I-9 (verify employment eligibility)
  - Form W-4 (withholding allowances)
  - New Hire Reporting (within 20 days)
  - FICA and federal withholding
  - FUTA (unemployment tax)
  - Workers' compensation insurance
  
- [ ] **State requirements:**
  - State withholding (if applicable)
  - State unemployment insurance
  - State new hire reporting
  - Workers' compensation
  - Disability insurance (CA, NY, NJ, RI, HI)
  
- [ ] **Employee policies:**
  - Employee handbook
  - At-will employment agreements
  - Confidentiality and IP assignment agreements
  - Non-compete/non-solicit (if enforceable)
  - Expense reimbursement policy
  - PTO policy
  - Remote work policy (if applicable)

---

### Securities Law Compliance

#### Stock Issuances
- [ ] **Comply with securities laws for all issuances**
  - Federal: Securities Act of 1933 (Rule 506(b) or 506(c) exemptions common)
  - State: Blue sky laws (may be preempted by federal exemption)
  
- [ ] **File Form D with SEC**
  - Required for Regulation D offerings
  - File within 15 days of first sale
  - File in each state where securities sold (check state requirements)
  
- [ ] **Accredited investor verification**
  - If raising funds, verify accredited investor status
  - Document verification method
  - Maintain records

#### Future Fundraising
- [ ] Prepare data room
- [ ] Update cap table
- [ ] Obtain 409A valuation (before option grants)
- [ ] Prepare financial statements
- [ ] Document all corporate actions
- [ ] Ensure all agreements are signed and filed

---

### Foreign Qualification

**If Doing Business in Other States:**
- [ ] Determine if foreign qualification required
  - Physical presence (office, employees)
  - Significant revenue from state
  - Owning/leasing property in state
  
- [ ] File Certificate of Authority in each required state
- [ ] Appoint registered agent in each state
- [ ] Comply with annual reporting in each state
- [ ] Pay applicable taxes and fees in each state

### Data Privacy and Security

**If Collecting Customer Data:**
- [ ] **Privacy policy** (required)
- [ ] **Terms of service** (required)
- [ ] **Cookie consent** (if EU visitors)
- [ ] **GDPR compliance** (if EU customers)

  - Right to deletion
  - Right to access

## Next Steps

### Immediate Actions (First 30 Days)
1. **Complete incorporation filing** - Get certificate of incorporation filed and approved
2. **File 83(b) elections** - CRITICAL DEADLINE: 30 days from stock grant
3. **Obtain EIN** - Required for bank account and all tax matters
4. **Hold organizational meeting** - Adopt bylaws, elect officers, issue stock
5. **Open bank account** - Establish corporate finances separate from personal
6. **Set up accounting** - Implement bookkeeping system from day one

### First Quarter Actions
1. **Register for state/local taxes** - Don't miss registration deadlines
2. **Obtain required licenses** - Research and apply early (can take weeks)
3. **Implement compliance calendar** - Track all filing deadlines
4. **Execute founder agreements** - Stock purchase, IP assignment, vesting
5. **Purchase insurance** - Don't operate uninsured
6. **Establish corporate records system** - Document everything from the start

### Ongoing (First Year)
1. **Maintain corporate formalities** - Hold meetings, keep minutes, document decisions
2. **File all required tax returns** - Federal, state, employment taxes
3. **Update cap table** - Keep accurate ownership records
4. **Protect IP** - File trademarks, patents; maintain confidentiality
5. **Prepare for fundraising** - Build relationships, refine pitch, update materials
6. **Annual compliance** - State reports, franchise tax, annual meeting

### Professional Advisors to Engage
- **Startup attorney** - Entity formation, contracts, fundraising
- **Tax accountant/CPA** - Tax strategy, compliance, bookkeeping
- **Financial advisor** - Budgeting, forecasting, fundraising preparation
- **Insurance broker** - Appropriate coverage for startup stage
- **Trademark attorney** - IP protection strategy (if significant IP)

### Resources
- **Clerky** - Automated incorporation and legal documents
- **Stripe Atlas** - Incorporation package with banking and tools
- **Gust Launch** - Incorporation and compliance platform
- **Carta** - Cap table management
- **Pulley** - Cap table and equity management
- **AngelList** - Fundraising and back office services

### Red Flags to Avoid
- ❌ Missing 83(b) election deadline
- ❌ Issuing stock without vesting
- ❌ No written agreements with co-founders
- ❌ Commingling personal and business finances
- ❌ No IP assignment agreements
- ❌ Failing to document board/stockholder actions
- ❌ Missing annual compliance deadlines
- ❌ Issuing stock without securities law compliance
- ❌ No founder vesting (investors will require fixing)
- ❌ Operating without proper insurance
