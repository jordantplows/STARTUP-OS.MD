## Overview

This document defines the OKR (Objectives and Key Results) framework for [PLACEHOLDER - Company Name], including the quarterly planning process, scoring methodology, and examples of well-written OKRs. OKRs are our system for setting ambitious goals, measuring progress, and aligning the entire company around what matters most.

### Purpose

- Align company, teams, and individuals around shared priorities
- Set ambitious, measurable goals that drive focus and accountability
- Create transparency across the organization (everyone sees everyone's OKRs)
- Enable quarterly planning and progress tracking
- Balance long-term vision with short-term execution

### What are OKRs?

**Objective:** The qualitative goal you want to achieve. It should be inspiring, ambitious, and directional.

**Key Results:** The quantitative metrics that measure progress toward the Objective. They should be specific, measurable, and time-bound.

**Example:**
- **Objective:** Become the leading solution for mid-market sales teams
- **Key Result 1:** Grow from 1,000 to 2,500 customers (+150%)
- **Key Result 2:** Achieve 95% gross revenue retention (up from 90%)
- **Key Result 3:** Reach $1M ARR from mid-market segment (up from $400K)

**[PRINCIPLE]** OKRs are stretch goals, not commitments. Achieving 60-70% of your OKRs is considered successful. If you're consistently hitting 100%, your OKRs aren't ambitious enough.

## OKR Framework

### OKR Hierarchy

**Company OKRs → Team OKRs → Individual OKRs**

**Company OKRs (3-5 per quarter):**
- Set by CEO + Leadership Team
- Reflect top strategic priorities for the company
- All teams align their OKRs to these

**Team OKRs (1-3 per quarter):**
- Set by Department Head + Team
- Directly support one or more Company OKRs
- Examples: Engineering OKRs, Sales OKRs, Product OKRs

**Individual OKRs (1-2 per quarter):**
- Set by individual contributor + manager in 1-on-1
- Support Team OKRs
- Include both business outcomes and personal growth goals

**[PRINCIPLE]** OKRs cascade. Every team and individual OKR should clearly map to a Company OKR. If it doesn't, question whether it's a priority.

### OKR Cycle

**Quarterly Cadence:**
- **Week 1 of Quarter:** OKR Planning (set OKRs for new quarter)
- **Weeks 2-12:** Execute against OKRs
- **Week 4 & 8:** Mid-Quarter Check-Ins (progress updates, adjust if needed)
- **Week 13:** OKR Scoring and Retrospective (evaluate previous quarter)

**[ASSUMPTION]** Quarters align with calendar year:
- Q1: January - March
- Q2: April - June
- Q3: July - September
- Q4: October - December

## Writing Good OKRs

### Characteristics of Strong OKRs

**Objectives:**
- ✅ Aspirational and inspiring (not just "business as usual")
- ✅ Qualitative and directional
- ✅ Time-bound (implied: this quarter)
- ✅ Memorable and easy to communicate
- ❌ Not a laundry list of tasks

**Key Results:**
- ✅ Quantitative and measurable (numbers, percentages, yes/no)
- ✅ Outcome-focused (not activity-focused)
- ✅ Ambitious but achievable (60-70% success is expected)
- ✅ Verifiable (clear data source to measure)
- ❌ Not vague or subjective ("improve quality" is too vague)

### Good vs. Bad OKR Examples

**❌ BAD Objective (Too Vague):**
- "Improve the product"

**✅ GOOD Objective (Specific and Inspiring):**
- "Deliver a best-in-class onboarding experience that drives customer success"

---

**❌ BAD Key Result (Activity, Not Outcome):**
- "Launch 5 new features"

**✅ GOOD Key Result (Outcome-Focused):**
- "Increase 30-day activation rate from 40% to 60%"

---

**❌ BAD Key Result (Not Measurable):**
- "Improve sales team effectiveness"

**✅ GOOD Key Result (Measurable):**
- "Increase average deal size from $5K to $8K"

---

**❌ BAD Key Result (Too Easy):**
- "Hire 1 engineer" (if you're already planning to do this)

**✅ GOOD Key Result (Ambitious):**
- "Hire 5 engineers and achieve 90% offer acceptance rate"

---

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid

**1. Confusing OKRs with Tasks**
- ❌ "Migrate database to AWS" (this is a task)
- ✅ "Improve system reliability to 99.9% uptime" (this is an outcome)

**2. Too Many OKRs**
- ❌ 10 Objectives with 40 Key Results (impossible to focus)
- ✅ 3-5 Objectives with 2-4 Key Results each (focused and manageable)

**3. Sandbagging (Setting Goals You've Already Achieved)**
- ❌ "Reach $500K ARR" (when you're already at $480K)
- ✅ "Reach $800K ARR" (when you're at $500K - ambitious but possible)

**4. No Connection to Company OKRs**
- ❌ "Redesign our homepage" (why? how does it support company priorities?)
- ✅ "Increase website conversion rate from 2% to 4% to support customer acquisition goal" (clearly tied to company OKR)

**5. Vanity Metrics**
- ❌ "Grow social media followers to 10,000" (followers don't drive business)
- ✅ "Generate 200 qualified leads from content marketing" (leads drive business)

## OKR Scoring Methodology

### Scoring Scale (0.0 - 1.0)

**0.0 - 0.3 (Red - Significantly Below Expectations):**
- Little to no progress made
- Likely due to misaligned priorities, lack of resources, or external blockers
- Requires discussion: Was this the right goal? What went wrong?

**0.4 - 0.6 (Yellow - Below Target but Progress Made):**
- Made meaningful progress but fell short
- This is expected for ambitious OKRs
- Learn from challenges and iterate

**0.7 - 1.0 (Green - Met or Exceeded Target):**
- Strong performance
- 0.7-0.9: Met ambitious goals
- 1.0: Exceeded expectations (celebrate, but also question if goal was ambitious enough)

**[PRINCIPLE]** Average OKR score should be 0.6-0.7 across the company. If consistently higher, goals aren't ambitious enough. If consistently lower, goals are unrealistic or resources are misallocated.

### How to Score Key Results

**Quantitative Key Results (Most Common):**

Formula: `Score = (Actual - Starting Value) / (Target - Starting Value)`

**Example:**
- Starting ARR: $500K
- Target ARR: $1M
- Actual ARR: $800K
- Score: ($800K - $500K) / ($1M - $500K) = $300K / $500K = **0.6**

**Boolean Key Results (Yes/No):**
- Did you complete it? Yes = 1.0, No = 0.0
- Example: "Launch mobile app by end of Q2" → Launched = 1.0, Not Launched = 0.0

**Percentage Key Results:**
- If target is a percentage, calculate based on actual vs. target
- Example: Target = 95% uptime, Actual = 97% uptime → Score = 1.0 (exceeded)
- Example: Target = 95% uptime, Actual = 90% uptime → Score = 0.0 (significant miss)

**Subjective Key Results (Avoid if Possible):**
- If you must use subjective KRs, define clear criteria in advance
- Example: "Achieve 8/10 CSAT score on new onboarding flow" (CSAT is measurable)

### Scoring Process

**Who Scores:**
- Individuals score their own OKRs (self-assessment)
- Managers review and validate scores in 1-on-1s
- Leadership Team scores Company OKRs collaboratively

**When:**
- Last week of quarter (Week 13)
- Final scores due before OKR Retrospective meeting

**Where:**
- OKRs tracked in Notion or Google Sheets (depending on company tool preference)
- Scores and commentary added to OKR doc

**[BEST PRACTICE]** Include commentary with scores. Don't just write "0.6" - explain what happened, what you learned, and what you'll do differently next quarter.

## OKR Planning Process

### Timeline

**Week 13 of Previous Quarter:**
- Score and retrospective on current quarter OKRs
- CEO drafts proposed Company OKRs for next quarter

**Week 0 (Between Quarters):**
- Leadership Team reviews and finalizes Company OKRs
- Department Heads draft Team OKRs aligned to Company OKRs

**Week 1 of New Quarter:**
- **Monday:** OKR Planning Meeting (Leadership Team, 2 hours)
  - Review and finalize Company OKRs
  - Review and align Team OKRs
- **Tuesday-Wednesday:** Department Heads share Team OKRs with their teams
  - Teams provide feedback and align on priorities
- **Thursday:** Individuals draft their OKRs with managers in 1-on-1s
- **Friday:** All OKRs finalized and published in Notion

**Week 2:**
- OKRs shared in All-Hands meeting
- Everyone has visibility into company, team, and individual OKRs

**[PRINCIPLE]** OKR planning is collaborative, not top-down. CEO sets direction with Company OKRs, but teams have autonomy to define how they'll contribute.

### OKR Planning Meeting Agenda (Week 1, Monday, 2 hours)

**Participants:** CEO, CTO, CPO, CFO, Department Heads

**Facilitator:** CEO

**Agenda:**
1. **Previous Quarter Review (30 min):**
   - Review Company OKR scores
   - What worked? What didn't?
   - Key lessons learned
2. **Set Company OKRs (60 min):**
   - CEO presents draft Company OKRs
   - Discuss and refine (are these the right priorities?)
   - Finalize 3-5 Company Objectives with Key Results
3. **Review Team OKRs (30 min):**
   - Each Department Head presents their proposed Team OKRs
   - Ensure alignment to Company OKRs
   - Identify dependencies across teams

**Outcomes:**
- Company OKRs finalized
- Team OKRs aligned and approved
- Dependencies identified and owners assigned

**Notes:** Saved to Notion `/company/okrs/[YEAR]-Q[N]/`

## OKR Check-Ins & Updates

### Mid-Quarter Check-Ins (Week 4 & 8)

**Purpose:** Review progress, identify blockers, adjust if needed

**Format:** Async updates + optional sync discussion

**Process:**
1. **Individuals update OKR progress in Notion** (by Monday 10 AM)
   - Current score estimate (0.0 - 1.0)
   - Progress update (1-2 sentences)
   - Blockers or risks (if any)
2. **Managers review and discuss in 1-on-1s** (if needed)
3. **Leadership Team reviews Company OKRs** (in weekly sync)
   - Are we on track?
   - Do we need to adjust priorities?
   - Do we need to reallocate resources?

**[POLICY]** OKRs are not set in stone. If business priorities shift mid-quarter, it's okay to adjust OKRs. Document the change and explain why.

### Weekly Progress (Optional)

**[OPTIONAL]** Some teams find it helpful to reference OKRs in weekly standups or sprint planning:
- "Which OKR does this sprint's work contribute to?"
- "Are we making progress on our Key Results?"

**[PRINCIPLE]** OKRs inform sprint planning, but they're not a replacement for backlogs or roadmaps. OKRs are the "what" and "why"; sprints are the "how."

## OKR Retrospective & Reflection

### Retrospective Process (Week 13)

**Purpose:** Reflect on OKR outcomes, learn from successes and failures, inform next quarter

**Format:** Written retrospective (Notion) + discussion (meeting or async)

**Questions to Answer:**

**1. Scoring & Results:**
- What was your final score on each Key Result?
- What contributed to success or failure?

**2. Learnings:**
- What surprised you this quarter?
- What would you do differently next time?
- What assumptions were wrong?

**3. Impact:**
- Did achieving (or not achieving) these OKRs move the needle on business outcomes?
- Were these the right priorities in hindsight?

**4. Next Quarter:**
- What should we continue doing?
- What should we stop or change?
- What new priorities should we focus on?

**[BEST PRACTICE]** Celebrate wins (even if scored 0.6) and learn from misses (without blame). The goal is continuous improvement, not perfection.

## OKR Best Practices

### Do's

✅ **Keep it simple:** 3-5 Objectives with 2-4 Key Results each  
✅ **Make it measurable:** Every Key Result should have a clear number or yes/no outcome  
✅ **Align across teams:** Every team OKR should support a company OKR  
✅ **Be ambitious:** 60-70% achievement is the target, not 100%  
✅ **Focus on outcomes, not tasks:** "Increase ARR" not "Launch feature X"  
✅ **Review regularly:** Mid-quarter check-ins keep OKRs top-of-mind  
✅ **Be transparent:** Everyone should see everyone's OKRs  
✅ **Learn from failures:** Low scores are valuable data, not punishments

### Don'ts

❌ **Don't set too many OKRs:** More than 5 Objectives = no focus  
❌ **Don't confuse OKRs with tasks:** OKRs are goals, not to-do lists  
❌ **Don't tie OKRs to compensation:** OKRs should be ambitious; tying to comp encourages sandbagging  
❌ **Don't set and forget:** OKRs need regular check-ins and updates  
❌ **Don't make them easy:** If you're hitting 100%, you're not stretching enough  
❌ **Don't use vanity metrics:** Focus on business outcomes, not activity  
❌ **Don't work in silos:** OKRs should drive cross-functional collaboration

## Common Questions

### Q: What's the difference between OKRs and KPIs?

**A:** KPIs (Key Performance Indicators) are ongoing metrics you track continuously (e.g., MRR, churn, uptime). OKRs are time-bound goals you set quarterly to drive improvement in specific areas. You might have a KPI for "MRR" that you track every month, and an OKR to "Grow MRR from $500K to $1M by end of Q2."

### Q: Should OKRs be tied to compensation or performance reviews?

**A:** No. OKRs should be ambitious stretch goals. If you tie them to compensation, people will sandbag (set easy goals to ensure they hit 100%). Performance reviews should consider effort, impact, and growth - not just OKR scores.

### Q: What if priorities change mid-quarter?

**A:** It's okay to adjust OKRs if business priorities shift. Document the change, explain why, and communicate to the team. Flexibility is important, but avoid changing OKRs too frequently (it undermines focus).

### Q: How do OKRs relate to sprint planning?

**A:** OKRs are the "what" and "why" (strategic goals). Sprints are the "how" (tactical execution). Sprint planning should prioritize work that moves the needle on OKRs. Ask: "Which OKR does this sprint's work support?"

### Q: What if I don't have a full quarter to work on an OKR (e.g., I join mid-quarter)?

**A:** Adjust the timeframe or scope. For example, set an 8-week OKR instead of 12-week. Or focus on a subset of the team's OKRs that you can meaningfully contribute to.

### Q: How many individual OKRs should I have?

**A:** 1-2 per quarter. One aligned to your team's OKRs (business impact), and optionally one focused on personal growth (e.g., "Complete certification", "Mentor 2 junior team members").
