### 2. [VALUE #2: e.g., "Customer Obsession"]

**What it means:**

[PLACEHOLDER: Define in 2-3 sentences. E.g., "We start with the customer and work backward. Every decision is filtered through: Does this make our customers' lives easier? We measure success by customer outcomes, not internal metrics."]

**What it looks like in practice:**

- **In product development**: [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "Product managers spend 5+ hours per week in customer calls or support tickets"]
- **In prioritization**: [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "We prioritize features based on customer impact, not how technically interesting they are"]
- **In communication**: [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "Customer emails get responses within 4 hours, not 'whenever we get to it'"]
- **In hiring**: [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "Every candidate talks to a customer during the interview process"]

**Real examples from our team:**

[PLACEHOLDER: 2-3 stories. E.g., "When Customer X mentioned in passing that exports were slow, Jordan spent the weekend optimizing it. No ticket, no sprint planning—he just fixed it because it mattered to them. Response time went from 30 seconds to 2 seconds."]

**What it is NOT:**

- Not saying yes to every customer request (we have a strategy)
- Not building one-off features for loud customers
- Not letting customers dictate our roadmap (we synthesize feedback into insights)
- Not being a doormat—we push back when customers are wrong

**How we reinforce it:**

- [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "We start every all-hands with a customer story, not our metrics"]
- [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "Engineers rotate through support every quarter"]
- [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "Our bonus structure includes NPS as a key metric"]

### 3. [VALUE #3: e.g., "Radical Transparency"]

**What it means:**

[PLACEHOLDER: Define in 2-3 sentences. E.g., "We default to sharing information, not hoarding it. We give each other direct, candid feedback—even when it's uncomfortable. We share financial metrics, strategic decisions, and mistakes openly."]

**What it looks like in practice:**

- **In internal communication**: [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "All Slack channels are public unless there's a specific reason to make them private"]
- **In feedback**: [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "We give feedback directly to the person, not through back-channels or gossip"]
- **In leadership**: [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "Our board decks are shared with the entire company within 48 hours of the meeting"]
- **In hiring**: [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "We share our hiring rubrics and interview questions publicly"]

**Real examples from our team:**

[PLACEHOLDER: 2-3 stories. E.g., "When we missed our Q2 revenue target by 20%, the CEO shared the full post-mortem with the team the next day—including what she personally could have done differently. No spin, no excuses."]

**What it is NOT:**

- Not oversharing personal information or creating drama
- Not giving feedback in public to embarrass someone (radical candor, not cruelty)
- Not sharing confidential customer data or violating privacy
- Not confusing transparency with consensus—leaders still make final calls

**How we reinforce it:**

- [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "We do monthly 'Ask Me Anything' sessions with the CEO"]
- [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "Our retros use 'Start/Stop/Continue' format with no topic off-limits"]
- [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "We have a #feedback channel where anyone can give public kudos or constructive input"]

### 4. [VALUE #4: e.g., "Ownership Mindset"]

**What it means:**

[PLACEHOLDER: Define in 2-3 sentences. E.g., "We act like owners, not renters. If something is broken, we fix it—even if it's not 'our job.' We think long-term, not quarter-to-quarter. We care about the whole company, not just our team."]

**What it looks like in practice:**

- **In problem-solving**: [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "If you see a bug, you fix it—or at minimum, file a ticket and assign it"]
- **In meetings**: [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "We say 'I'll handle it' instead of 'someone should do that'"]
- **In cross-functional work**: [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "Engineers jump into customer calls, sales reps suggest product improvements"]
- **In hiring**: [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "We ask: 'Tell me about a time you solved a problem that wasn't your responsibility'"]

**Real examples from our team:**

[PLACEHOLDER: 2-3 stories. E.g., "Our marketing manager noticed our onboarding emails had a broken link. Instead of Slacking someone, she opened a PR herself to fix it. Done in 10 minutes."]

**What it is NOT:**

- Not stepping on toes or undermining others' authority
- Not becoming a bottleneck by taking on too much
- Not ignoring your core responsibilities to chase shiny problems
- Not blaming others when things go wrong

**How we reinforce it:**

- [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "We offer equity to all employees, not just executives"]
- [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "Our performance reviews ask: 'What did you do outside your job description?'"]
- [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "We have a #fixit-friday tradition where anyone can work on anything they think is broken"]

### 5. [VALUE #5: e.g., "Default to Simple"]

**What it means:**

[PLACEHOLDER: Define in 2-3 sentences. E.g., "We favor simple solutions over complex ones. We cut features, not add them. We ask 'What can we remove?' before 'What should we build?'"]

**What it looks like in practice:**

- **In product development**: [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "Product specs include a 'What are we NOT building?' section"]
- **In code reviews**: [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "We challenge PRs that add complexity: 'Can this be simpler?'"]
- **In communication**: [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "Internal docs are 1-pagers, not 20-slide decks"]
- **In hiring**: [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "We ask candidates to explain a complex topic in simple terms"]

**Real examples from our team:**

[PLACEHOLDER: 2-3 stories. E.g., "Our dashboard had 15 widgets. Users were overwhelmed. We cut it down to 5 and satisfaction scores went up 30%."]

**What it is NOT:**

- Not dumbing things down or avoiding hard problems
- Not skipping necessary steps or cutting corners on quality
- Not confusing simple with easy (simple is often harder to achieve)
- Not ignoring edge cases that actually matter

**How we reinforce it:**

- [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "Every feature launch requires a 'complexity tax' review"]
- [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "We celebrate deleted code, not just new code"]
- [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "Our design reviews ask: 'Can we remove anything?'"]

## How We Hire for Culture

### We look for:

1. **Values alignment over resume pedigree**
   - [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "We'd rather hire someone with the right values and coach them on skills than hire a rockstar who doesn't fit"]

2. **Evidence, not claims**
   - [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "We ask behavioral questions: 'Tell me about a time when...' not 'Would you...'"]

3. **Culture add, not culture fit**
   - [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "We want people who bring new perspectives, not clones of existing team members"]

### Red flags:

- [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "Candidate blames others for past failures"]
- [PLACEHOLDER: E.g., "Candidate can't point to a time they changed their mind based on feedback"]
